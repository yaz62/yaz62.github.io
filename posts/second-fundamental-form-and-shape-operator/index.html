Second Fundamental Form, Shape Operator, And Curvature

July 9, 2025 5-minute read
Differential Geometry • Curvature • Fundamental Forms • Shape Operator

The second fundamental form and the shape operator are two concepts in differential geometry that are highly related and often confused. In this post, I will discuss the connection and differences between them, and how normal curvature emerges from these two concepts. But first, let me introduce the first fundamental form, first.

Given a parametrized surface $\mathbf{x}(u, v)$, and two vectors $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in T_PM$, the first fundamental form is the following quadratic form: $$ \mathrm{I}_P(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{u}\cdot \mathbf{v} $$ which looks trivial and unnecessary. Who needs a concept with such a fancy name only to perform a simple dot product after all? The idea is that, however, when you are given the coordinates of these two vectors using $\mathbf{x}_u$ and $\mathbf{x}_v$ as bases, the first fundamental form gives you a recipe for the dot product. Concretely, the matrix form of the first fundamental form (also often called the metric tensor) using $\mathbf{x}_u$ and $\mathbf{x}_v$ as bases is: $$ \mathrm{I}_P = \begin{bmatrix} E & F \\\ F & G \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}_u\cdot \mathbf{x}_u & \mathbf{x}_u\cdot \mathbf{x}_v \\\ \mathbf{x}_v\cdot \mathbf{x}_u & \mathbf{x}_v\cdot \mathbf{x}_v \end{bmatrix} $$ Now, if: $$ \begin{align*} \mathbf{u} &= u_1 \mathbf{x}_u + u_2 \mathbf{x}_v \\\ \mathbf{v} &= v_1 \mathbf{x}_u + v_2 \mathbf{x}_v \end{align*} $$ and for clarity, let: $$ \begin{align*} [u] &= [u_1, u_2] \\\ [v] &= [v_1, v_2] \end{align*} $$ then the following quadratic form spits out their dot product: $$ \begin{align*} \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} &= \Big(u_1\mathbf{x}_u + u_2\mathbf{x}_v\Big) \cdot \Big(v_1\mathbf{x}_u + v_2\mathbf{x}_v\Big) \\[1.5ex] &= [u_1, u_2] \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}_u\cdot \mathbf{x}_u & \mathbf{x}_u\cdot \mathbf{x}_v \\\ \mathbf{x}_v\cdot \mathbf{x}_u & \mathbf{x}_v\cdot \mathbf{x}_v \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\\ v_2 \end{bmatrix} \\[1.5ex] &= [u]^\intercal \mathrm{I}_P [v] \end{align*} $$ Note that $\mathrm{I}_P(\cdot, \cdot)$ is the bilinear function form of the first fundamental form, while $\mathrm{I}_P$ is its matrix form after choosing a set of basis vectors, and this notation will be kept for the second fundamental form, too. The first fundamental form defines distance and angle on the curved surface (intrinsic geometry), but it says nothing about curvature (extrinsic geometry), for which we turn to the second fundamental form. The second fundamental form is a quadratic form that defines how normal curvature along a normalized tangent vector $\mathbf{v}\in T_PM$ is calculated: $$ \mathrm{II}_P(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v}) = \pm \kappa $$ The $\pm$ is arbitary and depends on which side of the surface is considered “up”, which I’ll drop for simplicity. More generally, when the two inputs are different vectors of arbitary length $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\in T_PM$, the second fundamental form becomes: $$ \mathrm{II}_P(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = S_P(\mathbf{u})\cdot \mathbf{v} $$ where $S_P$ is the shape operator. Before we shift our focus to the shape operator, I’ll show the matrix form of the second fundamental form, using $\mathbf{x}_u$ and $\mathbf{x}_v$ as bases: $$ \mathrm{II}_P = \begin{bmatrix} l & m \\\ m & n \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}_{uu}\cdot \mathbf{n} & \mathbf{x}_{uv}\cdot \mathbf{n} \\\ \mathbf{x}_{uv}\cdot \mathbf{n} & \mathbf{x}_{vv}\cdot \mathbf{n} \end{bmatrix} $$ where $\mathbf n$ is the surface normal vector at $P$. So, $$ \begin{align*} \mathrm{II}_P(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) &= [u]^\intercal \mathrm{II}_P [v] \\[1.5ex] &= [u_1, u_2] \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}_{uu}\cdot \mathbf{n} & \mathbf{x}_{uv}\cdot \mathbf{n} \\\ \mathbf{x}_{uv}\cdot \mathbf{n} & \mathbf{x}_{vv}\cdot \mathbf{n} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\\ v_2 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*} $$

Next, I’ll explain three questions:

  • What is the shape operator?
  • How do we get the matrix form of the second fundamental form from the shape operator?
  • What is the formula for the normal curvature given a general tangent vector that is not normalized?

What is the shape operator Link to heading

Now, let’s shift our attention to the shape operator, which is at the core of understanding curvature. I’ll use Proposition 2.1 of Theodore Shifrin’s notes, Differential Geometry: A First Course in Curves and Surfaces, as the definition for the shape operator:

Definition. For any $\mathbf{v}\in T_PM$, the directional derivative $D_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{n}(P) \in T_PM$. Moreover, the linear map $S_P:T_PM \rightarrow T_PM$ defined by $$ S_P(\mathbf{v}) = -D_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{n}(P) $$ is a symmetric map; i.e., for any $\mathbf{u}$, $\mathbf{V} \in T_PM$, we have $$ S_P(\mathbf{u}) \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u} \cdot S_P(\mathbf{v}) $$ $S_P$ is called the shape operator at $P$.

The symmetry property can be shown easily, and the reader should feel free to try it. The physical image of the shape operator is clear: it’s the change in the surface normal vector in the direction of $\mathbf v$, up to a negative sign. This hints towards the concept of curvature: at a given point, in directions where curvatures are high, changes in the surface normal will also be large, and vice versa. However, the “change vector” resulted from the shape vector is still a vector, so to make it a scalar (as a very imprecise, hand-waving way to get to curvature…), curvature is defined as the projection of the “change vector” onto the direction vector $\mathbf{v}$ ($\lVert\mathbf{v}\rVert=1$): $$ \begin{align*} \kappa = S_P(\mathbf{v})\cdot \mathbf{v} \end{align*} $$

To be specific, we would like to make connections with the second fundamental form, and eventually, the normal curvature. Of course, as an operator, without selecting a set of basis vectors, it doesn’t have a matrix form readily for computation. Let’s see what happens if we apply it to $\mathbf{x}_u$, which is the default choice of basis for the tangent plane:

$$ \begin{align*} S_P(\mathbf{x}_u) = -D_{\mathbf{x}_u}\mathbf{n}(P) = -\mathbf{n}_u \end{align*} $$

Amazingly and expectedly, when the shape operator is applied to the tangent basis $\mathbf{x}_u$, we get the partial derivative $\mathbf{n}_u$, up to a negative sign. Intuitively, this makes sense: because $\mathbf{x}_u$ points in the direction of the $u$-curve of the surface, we get the change of the normal vector in the direction of the $u$-curve. Now, if we express $\mathbf{v} = a\mathbf{x}_u + b\mathbf{x}_v$: $$ \begin{align*} S_P(\mathbf{v})\cdot\mathbf{v} &= S_P(a\mathbf{x}_u + b\mathbf{x}_v) \cdot (a\mathbf{x}_u + b\mathbf{x}_v) \\[1.5ex] &= \Big(a S_P(\mathbf{x}_u) + b S_P(\mathbf{x}_v)\Big)\cdot (a\mathbf{x}_u + b\mathbf{x}_v) \\[1.5ex] &= - (a \mathbf{n}_u + b \mathbf{n}_v) \cdot (a\mathbf{x}_u + b\mathbf{x}_v) \\[1.5ex] &= [a, b] \begin{bmatrix} -\mathbf{x}_{u}\cdot \mathbf{n}_u & -\mathbf{x}_{u}\cdot \mathbf{n}_v \\\ -\mathbf{x}_{v}\cdot \mathbf{n}_u & -\mathbf{x}_{v}\cdot \mathbf{n}_v \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a \\\ b \end{bmatrix} \end{align*} $$

Given the coordinates on the tangent plane of any vector $\mathbf{v} \in T_PM$, the recipe for the directional curvature along $\mathbf{v}$ is: $$ \begin{align*} \kappa &= \frac{\mathbf{v}^\intercal \mathrm{II}_P \mathbf{v}}{\mathbf{v}^\intercal \mathrm{I}_P \mathbf{v}} \\\ &= \frac{[u_1, v_1] \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}_{uu}\cdot \mathbf{n} & \mathbf{x}_{uv}\cdot \mathbf{n} \\\ \mathbf{x}_{uv}\cdot \mathbf{n} & \mathbf{x}_{vv}\cdot \mathbf{n} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} u_1 \\\ v_1 \end{bmatrix}} {[u_1, v_1] \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}_u\cdot \mathbf{x}_u & \mathbf{x}_u\cdot \mathbf{x}_v \\\ \mathbf{x}_v\cdot \mathbf{x}_u & \mathbf{x}_v\cdot \mathbf{x}_v \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} u_1 \\\ v_1 \end{bmatrix}} \end{align*} $$
